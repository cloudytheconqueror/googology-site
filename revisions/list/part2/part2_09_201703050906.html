One thousand
1000 = 10^3

One thousand is the 10th cube (between 729 and 1331) and the cube of the base of our numeral system. Like 100, 1000 is given special names in most languages, such as "thousand" in English, "mille" in Latin, "chilia" in Greek, "qian" in Chinese, and "tysyacha" in Russian.

1000 is used as a base in Western numeral systems—most named numbers beyond 1000 are a power of 1000 (million = 10002, billion = 10003, etc.) and numbers with many digits often have their digits grouped by 3 to make it easier to read (for example 323,228,496 instead of 323228496).

A thousand could be considered a pretty big number, as a thousand things is pretty tricky to visualize in your head. However, it can still be considered to be a small number in some places. For example, a thousand dollars is a big amount of money (though it's still an everyday occurrence), while a thousand Korean won is worth about two-thirds of a package of snacks. For most people, thousand temporarily holds the honor for the largest number they've heard of, before being replaced by actual "really big numbers" such as a million.

The SI prefix for a thousand is kilo- (from Greek), and its inverse is milli- (from Latin), which both originate from words meaning "thousand". The prefix milli- is used in words like "millenium" and "millipede".

One thousand one
1001

One thousand one is a product of three consecutive primes: 1001 = 7*11*13. The sequence of such numbers starts with 30, 105, 385, 1001, 2431, 4199, 7429, 12,673, 20,677, 33,263, 47,027, 65,231, 82,861, ... (OEIS A046301) This leads to a test of whether a number is divisible by 7, 11 or 13: Detach the last 3 digits of the number and calculate the difference between it and the remaining digits. Repeat until the number is less than 1000, and check if that number is divisible by 7, 11 or 13. For example, with 22,993,047, 22,993 - 47 = 22,946, and 946 - 22 = 924. 924 is a multiple of 7 and 11 but not 13, so 22,993,047 is a multiple of 7 and 11 but not 13.

This property of 1001 also causes some primorials and highly composite numbers to have repeating digits: 30,030, 510,510 and 9,699,690 in case of primorials, and 720,720, 1,081,080, 1,441,440, 2,162,160, 2,882,880, 3,603,600, 4,324,320, 6,486,480, 7,207,200, 8,648,640, 10,810,800 and 14,414,400 in case of highly composite numbers.

1001 appears in many book titles, such as 1001 Arabian Nights, "1001 Facts About [insert topic here]", etc. See also 101.

One thousand twenty four / binary-guppyspeck
1024 = 2^10

1024 is the second dekeract (10-D cube), the fourth penteract (5-D cube), the 32nd square and the tenth power of 2. Powers of two have many unique properties, such as 2n being the number of subsets of a set with n elements, the sum of the numbers on the (n+1)-th row of Pascal's triangle is equal to 2n, and the reciprocals of the powers of two add up to 2.

The sequence of powers of two starts 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192, 16,384, 32,768, 65,536, 131,072, 262,144, 524,288, 1,048,576, 2,097,152, 4,194,304, 8,388,608, 16,777,216, 33,554,432, 67,108,864, 134,217,728, 268,435,456, 536,870,912, 1,073,741,824, 2,147,483,648, 4,294,967,296, 8,589,934,592, ... (OEIS A000079) Powers of two are a popular thing to memorize, and I've memorized up to 134,217,728 myself.

1024 is a special power of two for human usage, since it's 2 to the power of 10 and it's close to 1000, a power of ten. This means that every power of two in the sequence will be about a thousand times larger than the one that's ten terms before.

Since 1024 is close to 1000, the computer industry has adopted the prefixes kilo-, mega- etc. to refer to powers of 1024 rather than 1000 (for example, a kilobyte = 1024 bytes rather than 1000). To avoid confusion, the binary SI prefix system was created, which uses 1024 as the base. The prefixes in the system are (mirroring the kilo-, mega-, etc. prefixes):
  • kibi- = 2^10 = 1024
  • mebi- = 2^20 = 1,048,576
  • gibi- = 2^30 = 1,073,741,824
  • tebi- = 2^40 = 1,099,511,627,776
  • pebi- = 2^50 = 1,125,899,906,842,624
  • exbi- = 2^60 = 1,152,921,504,606,846,976
  • zebi- = 2^70 = 1,180,591,620,717,411,303,424
  • yobi- = 2^80 = 1,208,925,819,614,629,174,706,176
1024 can be called "binary-guppyspeck" using Saibian's nomenclature, which is formed by applying the binary- prefix to guppyspeck, which is in turn formed by applying the -speck suffix to guppy. Guppy is 10^20, the -speck suffix reduces the exponent by 10, and the binary- prefix changes the base to 2, so guppyspeck = 10^(20-10) = 10^10, and binary-guppyspeck = 2^10 = 1024.

One thousand seven hundred twenty nine / Hardy-Ramanujan number
1729

The number 1729 was made famous by a story involving Hardy and Ramanujan, both being early 20th century mathematicians. When Hardy visited Ramanujan who was in the hospital, he was picked up in a cab numbered 1729. Hardy told Ramanujan that 1729 isn't an interesting number, in which Ramanujan replied that he considered it an interesting number because it is the smallest positive integer that can be represented as the sum of two positive cubes in two non-trivial ways: 1729 = 123 + 13 = 1728 + 1, and 1729 = 103 + 93 = 1000 + 729.

A generalization of this idea leads to the taxicab numbers. The x-th taxicab number is the first positive integer expressible as a sum of two positive cubes in x different ways. The currently known taxicab numbers are 2, 1729, 87,539,319, 6,963,472,309,248, 48,988,659,276,962,496, and 24,153,319,581,254,312,065,344 (OEIS A011541).

If we allow the cubes to be zero or negative as well, we get the cabtaxi numbers (see 91). See 50 and 65 for a similar property with squares.

1729's property also makes it a near-miss to Fermat's Last Theorem: 103+93 is only 1 apart from 123. Fermat's Last Theorem is a famous theorem that states that for all integer n>2, there are no non-trivial integer solutions a, b, c to the equation an + bn = cn. It was conjectured by Pierre de Fermat in the 17th century and caused over 300 years of effort by mathematicians to prove it.

If a solution an + bn = cn existed for n = pq where p, q > 2, it could be rewritten as (ap)q + (bp)q = (cp)q. With this and the proof for n=4 (provided by Fermat himself), it can be shown that we only need to prove this problem for the odd prime exponents. Many specific cases for the problem was proven, such as the proof for n=3 by Euler and the proof up to n=4,000,000 using computer techniques, but the full proof wasn't given until 1994 by Andrew Wiles, whose proof utilized elliptic curves, something seemingly unrelated to the theorem.

Two thousand thirty eight
2038

The "year 2038 problem" is a problem similar to the Y2K problem. In the Unix time system which is used by many computers, time is internally stored as a 32-bit integer as the number of seconds since January 1, 1970, called the Unix epoch. On 2038-01-19 03:14:08 (UTC), 2,147,483,648 seconds would have passed since the Unix epoch, and the time value would overflow and roll over to -2,147,483,648, which translates to 1901-12-13 20:45:52 (UTC). Systems that work with future dates will encounter the problem quicker.

See also 2000, 2,147,483,647 and 292,277,026,596.

Two thousand forty seven
2047 = 2^11-1

2047 is the smallest Mersenne number with a prime exponent that's not prime (see 127 for more about Mersenne numbers/primes).

In ancient times, people thought that for every prime p, 2p-1 is prime. This holds for the first 4 primes: 22-1 = 3, 23-1 = 7, 25-1 = 31, 27-1 = 127 are all primes. However, the next term gives the smallest counterexample to the statement, as when p=11, 211-1 = 2047 = 23×89.

It can be shown that if c is composite and has a factor x larger than 1, then 2x-1 is a factor of 2c-1. So if the exponent is composite, the Mersenne number is also composite. But the opposite doesn't hold, as the above counterexample demonstrates.

See also 4181.

Three thousand one hundred twenty one
3121

The smallest solution to a famous problem called "Monkey and Coconuts":

In a desert island, five sailors collected coconuts and agreed to divide them at the next morning, then they went to sleep.
The first sailor wakes up and gives one to the monkey and divides the rest of the coconuts into five piles with exactly equal size. He takes one of the piles and leaves the rest back in one pile. The other four sailors do the same: gives one to the monkey, divides the rest to five equal piles, and takes one of them.
After all this, in the morning, the sailors divide the remaining pile into five piles with equal size. How many coconuts did they collect at the beginning?

There are more than one answer to the problem, but the smallest solution is 3121. In this case, the first sailor gives one to the monkey, takes (3121-1)/5 = 624 coconuts and leaves 624*4 = 2496, the second takes (2496-1)/5 = 499 and leaves 499*4 = 1996, the third takes 399 and leaves 1596, the fourth takes 319 and leaves 1276, and the fifth takes 255 and leaves 1020. In the morning, the remaining coconuts are divided into five piles, each with 204 coconuts.

For general cases with n sailors, the minimal solution is nn - n + 1 for odd n, and (2n-1)nn - n + 1 for even n.

Four thousand ninety eight
4098

4098 is the current lower bound to BB(5), defined using the busy beaver function, denoted BB(n) (also called Rado's sigma function Σ(n)). BB(5) is the lowest value of the busy beaver sequence that's currently unknown. This bound was found by Buntrock and Marxen in 1990.

Calculating the busy beaver numbers is a hard problem. For each n, there are 24n×(n+1)2n Turing machines to consider—in the case of BB(5), 220×610 = 63,403,380,965,376, or about 63 trillion, and each of them has to be tested to determine to halt or not. Sophisticated methods can be used to reduce the number of Turing machines and to test them, and as of today most of the 5-state machines have been shown to halt with 4098 or less nonzero symbols on the tape or never halt; only a few machines are left to be proven.

BB(5) is called "White whale" by the Zany Zoo project. The Zany Zoo project is a project by James Harland that gives busy beaver-related functions and numbers whimsical alliterative animal names, such as "frantic frog function" or FF(n) for the number-of-transitions function, "blue bilby" for BB(3), "ebony elephant" for BB(4), "white whale" for BB(5), and "demon duck of doom" for BB(6).

As a side note, Scott Anderson wrote "Ah yes, the early 1960’s were a more innocent age." on the "Who Can Name the Bigger Number?" essay, referring to the term "Busy Beaver". This shows that the word "beaver" has already become a sexual slang back in 1999.

Four thousand nine hundred
4900

A problem devised by Édouard Lucas asks the number of cannonballs that can be arranged in a square, as well as a square pyramid (1 at the top, 4 at second layer, 9 at third layer, and so on). The problem can be stated as the Diophantine equation x(x+1)(2x+1) = 6y2. The only answers (x,y) to the equation are (1,1) and (24,70). In other words, 4900 cannonballs can be arranged as a square with 70 balls on one edge, as well as a 24-layer square pyramid.

The fact that 24×(24+1)(2×24+1) = 702 also has a connection to the Leech lattice and the Monster group.

"Five thousand" is the largest number whose name contains no repeated letters, and is the longest such name at 12 letters. Smaller numbers with no duplicated letters are 0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 40, 46, 60, 61, 64, 80, 81 and 84.

5000 can be called dust mite-chunk, which is formed by combining dust mite (50,000) with the -chunk suffix (which divides the number by 10).

Five thousand forty
5040 = 7!

The factorial of 7 (between 720 and 40,320) is the smallest factorial that's divisible by all positive integers from 1 to 10. It's also a highly composite number (60 factors, between 2520 and 7560), and the largest factorial that's also highly composite.

It's also expressible as a product of consecutive integers in two different ways (see 720): 5040 = 2×3×4×5×6×7 = 7×8×9×10. One could multiply both sides of the equation for 720 (2×3×4×5×6 = 8×9×10) by 7 to get this equation.

Five thousand forty one
5041

The square of 71, and the largest known square number that has the form n!+1: 5041 = 712 = 7!+1, and by noting the appearances of the digits 7 and 1 on both sides one could write sqrt(7!+1) = 71.

Other numbers with this property are 25 = 52 = 4!+1 and 121 = 112 = 5!+1. Determining the solutions (n, m) for n! + 1 = m2 is called Brocard's problem and currently it's been shown that there are no further solutions such that n ≤ 109.

Five thousand two hundred eighty
5280

The number of feet in a mile, which is a pretty irregular value. This is because a mile used to be 5000 feet, then it changed to incorporate other length units used for agricultural purposes. The units that were used to create a mile are the follows:
  • 1 yard = 3 feet
  • 1 rod = 5.5 yards = 16.5 feet
  • 1 chain = 4 rods = 66 feet
  • 1 furlong = 10 chains = 660 feet
  • 1 mile = 8 furlongs = 5280 feet

Six thousand one hundred seventy four
6174

6174 is known as the Kaprekar's constant, which is the result of the following routine:
  1. Take a four-digit number that isn't a repdigit
  2. Order its digits in descending order and call the result a (using leading 0's if necessary, for example 378 becomes 0378)
  3. Write a's digits in reverse and call the result b
  4. Calculate a - b
  5. Go back to step 2 and repeat the process
Here's an example: starting with 5939, we have:
  1. 9953 - 3599 = 6354
  2. 6543 - 3456 = 3087
  3. 8730 - 0378 = 8352
  4. 8532 - 2358 = 6174
Here we reach 6174 in four steps. It has been shown that this routine reaches 6174 in at most seven steps.

If you did the process with 3-digit numbers, the equivalent constant is 495. For 2-digit numbers the process ends with a loop instead; and for numbers with 5 digits or more, several different end-results are possible (loops or single values; see OEIS A099009 and A099010).

For a similar process that involves addition instead of subtraction, see 196. Kaprekar's constant is not to be confused with Kaprekar numbers (see 45).

Six thousand five hundred sixty one / tetrafact
6561 = 4* = 9^4 = 3^8

Tetrafact is a googolism defined using mixed factorial, a function by SpongeTechX (a Googology Wiki user now renamed TechKon), which is defined as x* = (...((((1+2)*3)^4)^^5)...){x-3}x (note the left-to-right order; see 163). Tetrafact is 4* = ((1+2)*3)^4 = (3*3)^4 = 9^4 = 6561. It is the ninth tesseract (between 4096 and 10,000) and 3 to the 8th power (3^8).

Tetrafact is not to be confused with tethrafact, which is E100(#^^#)^#100 in Extended Cascading-E (a much, much larger number).

Seven thousand one hundred forty
7140

7140 is the largest number that's both triangular and tetrahedral. In 7140's case, it's the 119th triangular number and the 34th tetrahedral number. The only numbers with this property are 0, 1, 10, 120, 1540, and 7140 (OEIS A027568).

Because of the above property, 7140 is also a number that occurs 5 or more times in Pascal's triangle. Such numbers begin with 1, 120, 210, 1540, 3003, 7140, 11,628, 24,310, 61,218,182,743,304,701,891,431,482,520... (OEIS A003015) It has been shown that there are infinitely many such numbers. 1 occurs infinitely many times in Pascal's triangle, 3003 occurs 8 times, and all other known members of the sequence occur 6 times each.

8191 is the 5th Mersenne prime (M13 = 213-1, between 127 and 131,071), and the smallest Mersenne prime that isn't an exponent of another Mersenne prime (in other words, 28191-1 is composite).

Nine thousand
9000

The number 9000 is well-known as the "over 9000" Internet meme, which originated from an episode of Dragon Ball Z. In the episode, Vegeta said "It's OVER 9000!!!" as a response to Nappa who asked about what the scouter says about a character's power level. The original Japanese line said "it's higher than 8000!"; it was mis-translated into English.

As a reference to the meme, it's the starting entry of the "My number is bigger!" discussion thread/large number contest on the xkcd forums. The original poster of the thread said that anything over nine thousand is "[chirping] unimaginably huge".

Other unrelated uses of the number 9000 are the ISO 9000 family of standards and the HAL 9000.

Nine thousand one
9001

This number is often used when referencing the "it's over 9000" Internet meme. It's the smallest number larger than 9000, or to be precise, the smallest positive integer larger than 9000. There is no smallest real number larger than 9000, as you can make things like 9000 + 1/BIG FOOT.
