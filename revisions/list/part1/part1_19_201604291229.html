Negative infinity
-∞

Negative infinity is a quantity that is less than all real numbers. It is not very useful to googology as it's not on the real number line, but it shows up when calculating the logarithm of zero (in any base), and taking the double logarithm of one.

Negative BIG FOOT
-FOOT10(10^100)

This is the negative of what that is currently considered the largest named number, BIG FOOT. It is the result of applying the FOOT function to googol 10 times, where FOOT(n) is defined as the largest number definable in first-order oodle theory (FOOT for short) using x or less symbols. LittlePeng9 of Googology Wiki came up with the definition, and Sbiis Saibian suggested the name.

Negative Rayo's number
-Rayo(10^100)

The negative of the former largest named number, until BIG FOOT was defined. Rayo's number is defined as the smallest number larger than any number expressable in first-order set theory with a googol or less symbols. Despite the simple definition, there's no way to compute the number.

Negative Loader's number
-D5(99)

The negative of the largest named computable number, whose definition can be turned into (for example) a Turing machine instruction or a C code. Loader's number is the output of loader.c, a C program code written by Ralph Loader at the Bignum Bakeoff contest. The goal of the contest was to write a C code that produces the largest possible result on a theoretical infinite-memory machine. The C code should contain no more than 512 non-blank symbols.

Loader's program diagonalizes over the calculus of constructions, a powerful theory.

Negative godsgodgulus
-E100#{100}#100

The negative of a number around the limit of Saibian's Extended Cascading-E Notation (xE^), which is currently one of the largest numbers Saibian defined using the Extensible-E system (ExE). It is not the largest number Saibian named—see BIG FOOT for that.

Negative tethrathoth
-E100#^^#100

This the negative of a number around the limit of Saibian's Cascading-E Notation (E^), the 3rd part of the Extensible-E system. This number is much smaller than the above, but it's still large enough to be around the limit of Peano arithmetic.

Negative gongulus
-{10,10 (100) 2}

This number is the negative of a number that is much smaller than the absolute values of the previous entries, but is still really big. It is the result of solving a 100-dimensional 10×10×...×10 hypercube of 10's in extended array notation.

Negative gongulus was mentioned in Jonathan Bowers' article "Why Does God Exist?", to make a point that every number has "trueness". This results in negative gongulus the least number mentioned on Bowers' entire site.

Negative one
-1

This is a special negative number, and the negative of the most important number in mathematics.

Some example of -1's use in mathematics are that any nonzero number to the power of -1 is its reciprocal (x^(-1) = 1/x), the square of the imaginary unit i^2 is equal to -1, and the famous identity e^(iπ) = -1, which can be used to find out what taking a number to the power of a complex number means.

-1 plays a vital role in googology, as it's used to define the predecessor of any integer. It is used in the definition of the predecessor function:

P(n) = n-1

As well as almost all other googological functions, such as the hyper operator:

Rule 1. a {1} b = a + b
Rule 2. a {n} 1 = a (if n>1)
Rule 3. a {n} b = a {n-1} (a {n} (b-1)) (if n>1)

The subtraction of 1, which is equivalent to the addition of -1, is needed to force the evaluation of any expression back to the base case (Rules 1 and 2 in this case), so that the notation eventually terminates. Looking in this way -1 has some actual significance in googology.

Sbiis Saibian suggests this as a boundary between "small" negative numbers and "large" negative numbers. Any negative number less than -1 is "large" and any negative number more than -1 is "small" (i.e. close to 0).

Another fact: According to this blog post, infinity is less than zero. In fact, it's equal to -1.

Double logarithm of two
log(log(2)) = -0.521390227654...

This number, although negative, has an important role in googology. Say, you want to convert 2^3^4^5 into a power tower of 10's with a "smallish" number at the top. For an accurate conversion we use logarithms:

2^3^4^5
= (10^log(2))^3^4^5 — 10^(log(x)) = x by definition
= 10^(log(2)*3^4^5)
= 10^(10^log(log(2))*(10^log(3))^4^5)
= 10^10^(log(log(2))+log(3)*4^5)
~ 10^10^(-0.521390+0.477121255*1024)
~ 10^10^(-0.521390+488.572165)
= 10^10^488.050775

This shows that if you want to calculate the topmost exponent with the precision of at least one digit after decimal point, you need to take log(log(2)) into account. This could be a big deal, considering that an increase of the topmost exponent by 0.5 results in the number (approximately) being cubed:

10^10^(n+0.5) = 10^(10^n*10^0.5) ~ 10^(10^n*3.1622) = (10^10^n)^3.1622

Imagine a bucket containing 10^10^n grains of sand. Imagine there are 10^10^n of such buckets in a storage room, and imagine 10^10^n such storage rooms. That's about what's needed to have 10^10^(n+0.5) sand grains. That's the difference between 10^10^n and 10^10^(n+0.5). Although in practical calculations in googology we ignore these differences as those are insignificant, sometimes when detailed calculation is needed, it is necessary to consider small numbers like this.

Zero
0

We reached the important number 0, the only real number that's neither negative nor positive.

Sbiis Saibian considers zero as the smallest quantity possible, and negative numbers are considered "less than zero" rather than "smaller than zero".

It is the additive identity — x + 0 = 0 + x = x for any number x. For other operations it leads to some degenerate cases: x*0 = 0, x^0 = 1, x^^0 = 1, x^^^0 = 1 and so forth.

Zero is equal to the logarithm of 1 and the double logarithm of 10: log(1) = log(log(10)) = 0. For logarithms with other bases, the double logarithm of the base equals 0: log_b(log_b(b)) = log_b(1) = 0.

In order for algorithm to terminate, a minimum value is necessary for every argument, which is typically chosen as 1 or 0. Therefore zero has some importance in googology as a minimum value, such as in the Ackermann function, and the minimum value for an entry in a separator in Bowers' array notation. Zero is also the smallest ordinal, and f_0(x) in the fast-growing hierarchy is defined as x+1.

The name "Non-positive number range" was a bit inaccurate, as zero, a non-positive number, is not in it. But it's the only non-positive number that won't be in that range.

Okojo-ermine number
Oe ~ 1/{54,55 (1) 2}

This is a very small number, defined by the Japanese googologist Aeton (Aetonal on GWiki) in 2013.

"Okojo" is a Japanese word with two meanings: "ermine", an okojo in winter fur, and "stoat", an "okojo" in summer fur.

Define the following:
  • f(n) = x where x satisfies (10↑↑n)^(10↑↑n) = (10↑)^(n+2) x — here (10↑)^a b is a power tower of a 10's with b at the top (or Ea#b in Hyper-E)
  • f(1,1,□) = f(54,□)
  • f(1,□,n) = f(1/(f(1,□,n-1)),□) where 1/(f(1,□,n-1)) needs to be rounded off to an integer. It is unclear which way it rounds off to.
  • f(■,m,1,□) = f(■,m-1,54,□)
  • f(■,m,□,n) = f(■,m-1,1/(f(■,m,□,n-1)),□) — also needs to be rounded off
Where:
  • □ is zero or more 1's separated by commas
  • ■ is zero or more integers larger than or equal to 1, separated by commas
  • m > 1 and n > 1
Using the f function, the okojo numbers are defined as:
  • Oe(n) = f(1,1,...,1) with n+1 1's, Os(n) = 1/Oe(n)
  • Okojo-ermine number (Oe) = Oe(54)
  • Okojo-stoat number (Os) = Os(54) = 1/Oe (roundoff not necessary)
The number 54 often appears in the definition because o, ko, jo can be read as 0, 5 and 4, respectively (although we're stretching a bit on 4, which is read as yon).

Champernowne constant
0.123456789101112...

This irrational number is formed by combining the digits of the positive integers in base 10 into a decimal number. It is proven to be normal in base 10, i.e. all strings of digits of length n has the same chance (1/10^n) of appearing in the decimal expansion of this number. For example, 458 is as likely to appear in the decimal expression as 719. But it might not be normal in other bases, where you change 10 to b and 1/10^n to 1/b^n. Very few specific numbers have been shown to be normal in all bases, although it was proven that almost all real numbers are normal in all bases.

Of particular note is the continued fraction of the constant, as it gives some large numbers. It starts with:

0; 8, 9, 1, 149083, 1, 1, 1, 4, 1, 1, 1, 3, 4, 1, 1, 1, 15, ... (OEIS A030167)

The 4th term (counting the 0 at the beginning as the 0th term) is 149,083, already a nice big number. The 18th term (the term after the 15) is 457540111...783010987, a number with 166 digits. The 40th term (the next large term) is even larger: 445735380...113172423 with 2504 digits.

Common logarithm of two
log(2) = 0.301029995664...

A small number that raising 10 to the power of this number results in 2. This number has an important role in googology. Since 10^log(2) = 2, it can be used to change the base of a power tower from 2 to 10. For example, when we calculate 2^2^30:

2^2^30
= 2^1,073,741,824
= (10^log(2))^1,073,741,824
= 10^(log(2)*1,073,741,824)

... and putting log(2)*1,073,741,824 in a calculator gives 323,228,496.622955. Therefore...

2^2^30 ~ 10^323,228,496.622955

This shows that this number (and other logarithmic numbers) is useful for changing the power's base from 2 to 10 to determine where powers of numbers other than 10 fall between the powers of 10. The negative number log(log(2)) is another number with a similar role in googology.

The logarithms of prime numbers are also useful for computing logarithms of composite numbers by factorizing them and by taking the sum of the logarithms of the factors. For example we can compute the logarithm of 32 using the logarithm of 2:

log(32) = log(2^5) = log(2)*5 = 0.301029996*5 = 1.50514998

If we knew logarithms of more prime numbers (such as log(3) and log(5)) we could compute the logarithm of even more numbers.

Common logarithm of three
log(3) = 0.47712125472...

This number satisfies the equality 10^log(3) = 3. Using log(2) and log(3), we can estimate the logarithm of some positive integers without a calculator. For example:

log(108) = log(2^2*3^3) = log(2^2) + log(3^3) = 2*log(2) + 3*log(3) = 2*0.30103 + 3*0.47712 = 0.60206 + 1.43136 = 2.03342

So in this way you can estimate the logarithm of any 3-smooth number (numbers with only 2 and 3 as prime factors). In fact, we could do even better; see the entry for log(5).

Euler-Mascheroni constant
0.5772156649...

This is the Euler-Mascheroni constant, denoted by the Greek lowercase letter gamma (γ).

One
1

After all these small numbers, we finally reached what's arguably the most important number in mathematics.

One is the smallest positive integer, and is the only positive integer that's neither prime nor composite. It is the multiplicative identity: x*1 = 1*x = x for all real number x.

Adding 1 could be considered the "zeroth operation" — the successor function S(n) satisfies S(n) = n+1, and it's considered one of the fundamental functions of googology.

It's the (trivial) output of many googological functions, such as BB(1) (busy beaver function), G(1) (Goodstein function), Hydra(1) (Kirby-Paris hydra), BH(2) (Buchholz hydra), TREE(1) (TREE function), and Ξ(1) (Xi function). The empty array {} evaluates to 1 in BEAF.

For positive integer n, 1^n, 1^^n, 1^^^n etc. all equal 1, and n^0, n^^0, n^^^0 etc. all equal 1. Also, n^1, n^^1, n^^^1 etc. all equal n.

1 is also often used as a minimum or default value for arguments in googological functions, such as BEAF, Hyper-E notation, and chained arrow notation.

Saibian defines "small number" as numbers smaller than 1. In other words, this number is "the smallest number that's not small", or an "infinitely small large number". One can be considered the smallest googolism, since googolisms are names for large numbers. Smaller numbers with names are called "micronyms" instead (see googol-minutia-speck).

This constant, denoted with the Greek letter phi (φ), is called the golden ratio and it's one of the most well-known constants in mathematics. It's the average of 1 and the square root of 5.

The ratio between two consecutive terms of the Fibonacci sequence converges to φ. In fact, even if we started with any number a_0 and a_1 (besides a_0 = a_1 = 0), and make a Fibonacci sequence with it (a_n = a_(n-1) + a_(n-2)), the ratio a_n/a_(n-1) converges to φ.

2

The number two is the first positive integer that comes after 1. In other words, 2 is S(1) where S(x) is the successor function. Since it is the first integer larger than one it could truly be called a "large number".

Two is the first prime number, and the only even prime number. All even numbers are multiples of 2.

2+2, 2*2, 2^2, 2^^2, 2^^^2 and so on all equal 4. This number has many other trivial properties, such that it's the output of SSCG(0) (simple subcubic graph number), q(1) (Laver table), tree(1) (weak tree function), and Ξ(2) (Xi function). It can be named "googoi" in the googo-naming system. It's also the third Fibonacci number.

A less trivial property is that in Bowers' linear array notation, arrays with 4 or more entries with its first entry equal to 2 will solve to 4, as it would turn into 2^^...^^2 with any number of up-arrows. In Conway's chained arrow notation, expressions starting with 2→2→ evaluate to 4.

Unlike 1, when you see two objects you are reminded of the number two. Two appears when there's a dichotomy (two types of things): male and female, quiet and loud, analog and digital, small and big, hot and cold, long and short, etc. Also, the word "both" is a special word for two things with no equivalent terms for groups with more than two objects.

A well-known usage of two is in binary, which a number is described by only using the digits (bits) 0 and 1. It is widely used in computing, where 0 could be represented as "off" and 1 as "on".

"Palpable" numbers are numbers small enough to be recognized immediately. You can fully wrap your mind around 3 or 6 things, but not in larger numbers like a thousand. In other words, these numbers able to be touched or felt (that's what palpable means).

e
2.718281828459...

A very well-known irrational (and transcendental) constant. It's also called Euler's number after Leonhard Euler, who made the constant famous.

It can be defined as the limit of (1+1/x)^x as x tends to infinity. e can also be calculated as the sum 1 + 1/1! + 1/2! + 1/3! + ... or the sum of the reciprocals of factorials. In fact ex can be calculated as 1 + x/1! + x^2/2! + x^3/3! + ...

The derivative of the function ex is ex (the same function). This means that the slope at any point along the y=ex curve is equal to the y-coordinate of that point. In particular, the slope at x=0 is 1. In fact e can be defined as the number a such that the slope of y=ax at x=0 is 1.

e appears many, many times in calculus, and appears a few times in googology, such as when studying infinite power towers, Stirling's approximation on large factorials, and the definition of Skewes' number.
